How Your HVAC System Works

Your HVAC system moves air through your home while heating or cooling it using a continuous cycle of airflow and heat exchange.

  • Thermostat: Signals the system to turn on when temperature changes
  • Indoor Unit: Heats or cools air using coils and a blower
  • Outdoor Unit: Releases or absorbs heat through the condenser
  • Ductwork: Distributes conditioned air throughout your home

Technician Tip: Always verify the simplest causes first before assuming major component failure. Restricted airflow, dirty filters, or thermostat issues are often overlooked and can mimic more serious system problems. When in doubt, perform a full system check to avoid unnecessary repairs.

Field Note ΓÇö Electrical

Electrical issues involve components such as capacitors, contactors, relays, and wiring. Symptoms like system failure to start, intermittent operation, or breaker trips are often tied to electrical faults. These components can carry high voltage and should typically be handled by a licensed technician.

Field Note ΓÇö Structural

Structural issues relate to the physical setup of the HVAC system, including ductwork, mounting, drainage, and airflow pathways. Problems like leaks, poor airflow distribution, or system strain can result from improper installation or blockages. Some issues may be visually identifiable and safe to address, while others require system-level adjustments.

Field Note ΓÇö Chemical

Chemical-related issues usually involve refrigerant levels, airflow contamination, or system pressures. Low refrigerant, leaks, or improper charge can reduce cooling efficiency and damage the compressor over time. These systems require specialized tools and certification to service safely and correctly.

Field Note ΓÇö Mechanical

Mechanical issues involve moving parts such as blower motors, condenser fans, belts, and bearings. Symptoms like grinding noises, weak airflow, or vibration often originate from mechanical wear or component failure. Some minor issues may be visible externally, but internal failures typically require professional inspection.

When to Call a Professional Technician

HVAC systems utilize dangerous components. Stop DIY efforts and call a pro immediately if you encounter:

  • ElectricalContactors, capacitors, control boards require LOTO training.
  • RefrigerantEPA Section 608ΓÇöillegal to vent or handle without license.
  • GasNever modify furnace gas valves or heat exchangers.

General guideline: DIY repairs may void manufacturer warranties, create unseen safety hazards, or cause unintended damage to your equipment. Work involving electrical (including high voltage), gas lines, or refrigerant handling is strongly discouraged for homeownersΓÇöand in many cases is illegal without proper certification (e.g., EPA Section 608 for refrigerants). When in doubt, consult a licensed HVAC professional.

When should I call a professional?

Call a pro if you suspect refrigerant leaks, electrical faults, or compressor issues. EPA Section 608 requires certification for refrigerant work.
